# Thailand Clinical Pathway: Dengue Fever / Dengue Hemorrhagic Fever (DHF)

## Source
- Department of Medical Services (กรมการแพทย์), MoPH — Clinical Practice Guideline for Dengue Hemorrhagic Fever (ฉบับย่อ) พ.ศ. 2566
- WHO/SEARO Dengue Guidelines (2011 revision)
- Queen Sirikit National Institute of Child Health (WHO Collaborating Centre for Dengue)

## 1. Classification System

### WHO 1997 Classification (Used in Thailand)
Thailand uses the WHO 1997 DF/DHF/DSS classification:
- **Dengue Fever (DF)** — ICD-10: A90 — fever + 2 of: headache, retro-orbital pain, myalgia, arthralgia, rash, hemorrhagic manifestations, leukopenia
- **Dengue Hemorrhagic Fever (DHF)** — ICD-10: A91 — all 4 criteria must be met:
  1. Fever (2-7 days, acute onset, high, continuous)
  2. Hemorrhagic manifestations (positive tourniquet test, petechiae, mucosal/GI bleeding)
  3. Thrombocytopenia (platelet ≤100,000/mm³)
  4. Plasma leakage (hematocrit rise ≥20%, or pleural effusion/ascites)

### DHF Severity Grading
- **Grade I**: Fever + non-specific symptoms; tourniquet test positive (only hemorrhagic manifestation)
- **Grade II**: Grade I + spontaneous bleeding (skin, mucosal, or other sites)
- **Grade III** (DSS): Circulatory failure — rapid/weak pulse, narrow pulse pressure (≤20 mmHg), hypotension, cold/clammy skin, restlessness
- **Grade IV** (DSS): Profound shock — undetectable pulse and blood pressure

### Modified WHO/SEARO 2011 Criteria
Major criterion: plasma leakage — elevation of Hct ≥20%, or detection of ascites/pleural effusion by physical exam, chest film (right lateral decubitus), or ultrasound.
Minor criteria: bleeding or positive tourniquet test; platelet ≤100,000/mm³.

## 2. Clinical Phases

### Phase 1: Febrile Phase (Day 1-7, typically 2-7 days)
- Acute high continuous fever (39-40°C)
- Facial flushing, skin erythema
- Generalized body aches, myalgia, arthralgia
- Headache, retro-orbital pain
- Anorexia, nausea, vomiting
- **Lab**: WBC tends to decrease; platelet begins declining by day 3-4
- **Management**: Supportive — oral fluids, paracetamol ONLY (NO aspirin, NO NSAIDs)

### Phase 2: Critical/Plasma Leakage Phase (24-48 hours around defervescence)
- Onset: typically day 3-7 of illness, when fever breaks
- Plasma leakage → hemoconcentration, pleural effusion, ascites
- Platelet nadir (<50,000 most likely DHF)
- **Risk of shock**: highest during this phase
- Duration: 24-48 hours
- **Close monitoring required**: vitals q1-4h, serial hematocrit

### Phase 3: Convalescence/Recovery Phase (2-5 days)
- Fluid reabsorption from extravascular spaces
- Risk of fluid overload if IV fluids not reduced
- Improvement in appetite, bradycardia
- Convalescence rash (confluent petechiae with pale islands)
- Platelet count recovery
- Hematocrit stabilizes or may drop (hemodilution)

## 3. Admission Criteria
Hospitalize if ANY of the following:
- Platelet ≤100,000/mm³ with clinical deterioration or poor oral intake
- Rising hematocrit (≥10-20% above baseline)
- Warning signs: severe abdominal pain, persistent vomiting, any bleeding, restlessness/lethargy
- Pleural effusion or ascites on examination/imaging
- Any signs of circulatory compromise
- High-risk patients: infants, obese, pregnant, elderly, underlying chronic diseases
- All DHF Grade II or higher MUST be admitted
- DHF Grade I: admit if patient cannot maintain adequate oral intake or close follow-up not possible

## 4. Diagnostic Workup

### Initial Assessment (All suspected dengue)
- Complete blood count (CBC) with differential — MANDATORY for fever ≥3 days
- Tourniquet test (positive: ≥20 petechiae per 1 inch² area)
- NS1 antigen (most sensitive days 1-5)
- Dengue IgM/IgG serology (useful after day 5)

### Monitoring During Admission
- Serial hematocrit: every 4-6 hours during critical phase; every 6-12 hours during febrile phase
- Platelet count: daily during febrile phase; every 6-12 hours during critical phase
- Vital signs: every 1-4 hours depending on severity
  - Grade I-II: every 4 hours
  - Grade III-IV (shock): every 15-30 minutes until stable, then every 1-2 hours
- Urine output monitoring: target ≥0.5 ml/kg/hr (adults), ≥1 ml/kg/hr (children)

### Conditional Tests (Based on Severity)
- Liver function tests (AST/ALT): if hepatomegaly, jaundice, or suspected liver involvement
- Coagulation profile (PT/PTT/INR): if significant bleeding or Grade III-IV
- Blood gas (capillary/venous): if shock or respiratory distress — assess metabolic acidosis
- Electrolytes (Na, K, Ca): if shock or prolonged IV therapy
- Blood glucose: if altered consciousness
- Blood type and crossmatch: if significant bleeding anticipated
- Chest X-ray (right lateral decubitus): detect pleural effusion
- Abdominal ultrasound: detect ascites, gallbladder wall thickening, pleural effusion
- Blood culture: if secondary bacterial infection suspected
- Dengue PCR/viral typing: if available, for epidemiological purposes

## 5. Drug Protocols

### Antipyretics
- **Paracetamol (acetaminophen) ONLY**: 10-15 mg/kg/dose every 4-6 hours (max 60 mg/kg/day in children, 4g/day in adults)
- **ABSOLUTELY CONTRAINDICATED**: Aspirin (increases bleeding risk), NSAIDs (ibuprofen, diclofenac, mefenamic acid — increase bleeding and may worsen platelet dysfunction)
- Tepid sponging for fever management

### IV Fluid Management

#### Non-Shock DHF (Grade I-II)
- Total fluid: Maintenance + 5% deficit (M+5%D) over 48 hours, including oral and IV
- **Isotonic crystalloids**: 5% Dextrose in Normal Saline (D5NSS), 5% Dextrose in Ringer's Lactate (D5RL), or Ringer's Acetate
- Initial rate based on ideal body weight (children):
  - <7 kg: 6 ml/kg/hr
  - 7-11 kg: 5 ml/kg/hr
  - 12-18 kg: 4 ml/kg/hr
  - >18 kg: 3 ml/kg/hr
- Adjust rate based on serial hematocrit and clinical response
- Duration: typically 24-48 hours through critical phase
- Reduce rate progressively once: platelets rising, hematocrit stable/falling, good urine output

#### Dengue Shock Syndrome (Grade III)
- **Initial resuscitation**: 10 ml/kg/hr isotonic crystalloid (NOT 20 ml/kg/hr like other types of shock — lower rate in DHF)
- If blood pressure improves within 1 hour: reduce to 7-10 ml/kg/hr, then taper over 24-36 hours
- If no improvement after 1-2 hours crystalloid: switch to **colloidal solution** (Dextran-40 10% in NSS: 10 ml/kg/hr bolus)
- Monitor hematocrit 2-4 hourly during shock management

#### Profound Shock (Grade IV)
- More aggressive fluid resuscitation
- Investigate and correct **ABCS**:
  - **A**cidosis: correct if pH <7.35, HCO3 <15 mEq/L (sodium bicarbonate)
  - **B**leeding: blood transfusion (fresh whole blood or packed RBC) if hematocrit not rising despite fluids
  - **C**alcium: calcium gluconate 1 ml/kg/dose IV slowly; correct Na/K abnormalities
  - **S**ugar: maintain blood glucose >60 mg/dL (IV dextrose)

### Blood Products
- Fresh whole blood or packed RBC: if estimated blood loss >6-8 ml/kg in children (hematocrit not rising or dropping with fluids)
- Platelet transfusion: NOT given prophylactically; only for significant active bleeding with severe thrombocytopenia
- Fresh frozen plasma: minimal role in acute management

### Medications for Complications
- **Furosemide**: 1 mg/kg/dose IV for fluid overload (after leakage phase resolves)
- **Vitamin K1**: 3-10 mg IV (age-dependent dose) for coagulopathy
- **Lactulose**: 5-10 ml every 6 hours for hepatic encephalopathy
- **Dexamethasone**: 0.5 mg/kg/day for elevated intracranial pressure (encephalitis complication)
- **Diazepam/Phenobarbital**: seizure management
- Steroids: NO role in routine DSS management

## 6. Length of Stay Guidelines

| Diagnosis | ICD | Grade | Min Days | Typical Days | Max Days | Notes |
|-----------|-----|-------|----------|-------------|----------|-------|
| Dengue fever (classic) | A90 | DF | 2 | 3 | 5 | Uncomplicated; admission for observation during critical phase |
| DHF Grade I | A91 | I | 3 | 4 | 5 | Monitor through critical phase; platelet recovery |
| DHF Grade II | A91 | II | 3 | 5 | 7 | Active bleeding management; longer monitoring |
| DSS Grade III | A91 | III | 5 | 7 | 14 | Shock resuscitation + recovery; ICU may be required |
| DSS Grade IV | A91 | IV | 7 | 10 | 21 | Profound shock + multi-organ complications; ICU required |

## 7. Discharge Criteria
ALL of the following must be met:
- Afebrile for >24-48 hours without antipyretics
- Improving clinical condition (good appetite, no distress)
- Rising platelet count (≥50,000 on upward trend)
- Stable or decreasing hematocrit (no further leakage)
- >30 hours after recovery from shock (if Grade III)
- >60 hours after resolution of plasma leakage
- Adequate urine output
- No respiratory distress (pleural effusion resolving)
- No bleeding for at least 24 hours
- Able to tolerate oral fluids and food

## 8. Warning Signs for Re-Admission
Patients discharged or being monitored as outpatient must return immediately if:
- Severe abdominal pain or persistent vomiting
- Any bleeding (gum bleeding, nosebleed, blood in stool/urine, vaginal bleeding)
- Change in mental status (restlessness, drowsiness, confusion)
- Cold, clammy extremities
- No urine output for 4-6 hours
- Difficulty breathing
- Worsening symptoms despite fever resolution

## 10. Complications to Watch For
- **Fluid overload**: cough, dyspnea, wide pulse pressure, tense abdomen (occurs in recovery phase if IV fluids not tapered)
- **Hepatic failure**: elevated AST/ALT >1000, hepatomegaly, encephalopathy
- **Encephalitis/Encephalopathy**: altered consciousness, seizures
- **Myocarditis**: arrhythmias, heart failure
- **Secondary bacterial infection**: recurrence of fever after afebrile period
- **Disseminated intravascular coagulation (DIC)**: prolonged PT/PTT, severe bleeding

## 11. Special Populations

### Pediatric Considerations
- Most common cause of pediatric hospitalization for infectious disease in Thailand
- Fluid calculations strictly based on ideal body weight
- Higher risk of shock in children aged 5-15 years
- Queen Sirikit National Institute of Child Health protocols are the national reference standard

### Pregnant Women
- Higher risk of severe disease and adverse pregnancy outcomes
- Fluid management must account for increased blood volume
- Avoid dehydration and fluid overload carefully
- Platelet thresholds for delivery: ≥50,000 for vaginal, ≥80,000 for C-section

### Elderly and Comorbid Patients
- Lower threshold for admission
- Careful fluid management (cardiac and renal comorbidities)
- Higher risk of atypical presentations
